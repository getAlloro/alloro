# Pete Blaber, Mentor Profile

*The first grounded mentor-profile, and the proof-of-concept for the mentor-advisory: each principle is tied to the real DECISION it came from and the negative knowledge (when it FAILS), never a slogan list. That grounding is the difference between an intern reciting "the 3 Ms" and a mentor who knows the judgment underneath.*

**Who he is (grounding):** Lt. Col. Pete Blaber, retired Delta Force officer. In Afghanistan (2001-02) he commanded AFO (Advanced Force Operations), the deep-recon element ahead of the main force: the Tora Bora hunt, Operation Anaconda, and the Battle of Takur Ghar (Roberts Ridge). Book: *The Mission, the Men, and Me* (2008). His throughline is NOT operator-badassery, it is **common sense as a disciplined decision method under uncertainty.** Evidence graded `[Documented]` (book / his site / on-record interview) vs `[Softer]` (reputable summary or paraphrase).

**The one-line system:** common sense (ego removed, #8, structurally guaranteed by Me-last, #1) lets you develop the situation (#3) by listening to the guy on the ground (#2) and sharing that reality boundarylessly (#5), while imagination (#7) widens options and chihuahua-discipline (#4) stops over/under-reaction on false patterns, so you plan continuously and accept intelligent risk (#6). **Each virtue over-applied becomes its own vice, the calibration IS the mentorship.**

---

## 1. The 3 Ms: Mission, Men, Me (in that order) `[Documented]`
- **Framing:** rank your motivations when they conflict, Mission first (and it must be legal/moral/ethical), the Men second (welfare and courage), Me dead last.
- **Story:** Northern Iraq, ordered to keep pressing an assault against a superior force. He read the mission as not worth the cost and ordered a tactical withdrawal, the real objective still ranked first, his men would not be spent needlessly, and the career cost to himself ranked last so it did not enter the calculation.
- **Judgment-pattern:** it is a tiebreaker that DELETES self-interest from the decision variables. Fear-for-self (ego, career, comfort, looking good) is the single biggest distortion on a hard call. Pre-ranking Me last removes it, so the call is cleaner, not just nobler.
- **When it fails:** "Me last" is about self-interest in the decision, NOT self-neglect as a lifestyle (a burned-out leader fails the mission too). It presumes a worthy mission, strip the moral filter and "mission first" becomes a machine for waste or atrocity. And bad bosses weaponize "mission first" to serve their OWN ego dressed as the mission, the tell is whose Me is actually being served.
- **For us:** run every hard call (a refund, a churn, a pivot) as mission > the two people > the founder's ego. In a tiny company the founder's ego is the most expensive variable, there is no org layer to absorb it. For agents: encode Mission > Men > Me as the arbitration rule when objectives conflict.

## 2. Always listen to the guy on the ground `[Documented]`
- **Framing:** the person in direct contact holds tacit, context-rich knowledge that cannot be transmitted by slide or phone. "Most of what we think we know will turn out to be incorrect or incomplete" until ground truth arrives.
- **Stories:** a park ranger / Vietnam vet gave his team more actionable terrain intel in one conversation than months of formal research (Bob Marshall Wilderness training). At Anaconda Day 1, Blaber, NOT even in the formal chain of command, argued over the radio that aborting would waste a battlefield opportunity, and Gen. Hagenbeck trusted the guy on the ground over the org chart.
- **Judgment-pattern:** push the decision to the point of maximum CONTEXT, not maximum RANK. Authority and information sit in different places, good systems route the decision to the information.
- **When it fails:** the ground operator has the best local context but the NARROWEST aperture (can't see the theater or second-order effects), and can be wrong, panicked, or biased by proximity. "Listen to" is not "always obey." And it fails without shared reality (#5), push decisions down without common understanding and you get fast WRONG decisions.
- **For us:** the core delegation rule. The support person who talks to customers, or the agent parsing every ticket, sees what the founder cannot, route those calls to them. But keep the whole-company-aperture calls (pricing, positioning, who to fire) human. Agents are literally guys-on-the-ground: huge local aperture, zero strategic aperture.

## 3. When in doubt, develop the situation `[Documented]` (his most transferable idea)
- **Framing:** instead of forcing a decision from incomplete info (or defaulting to a canned SOP), actively build context over time and keep options open until the situation reveals the move. He calls it "enlightened procrastination," treating life as a movie not a snapshot, time is an ally. Mental process: saturate → incubate → illuminate. It buys innovation, adaptation, audacity.
- **Roots he names:** Boyd's OODA loop (Orient is the decisive step) fused with Gary Klein's recognition-primed decision-making (experts pattern-match rather than compare options). Developing the situation feeds Orient and grows the pattern library.
- **Story/contrast:** the "helicopter-centric plan" (let the platform drive the mission: "we have helos, so it's a helo assault") is deciding from a snapshot and a default SOP. His AFO teams instead moved quietly, gathered local truth, and narrowed the target by elimination.
- **Judgment-pattern:** distinguish decisions that must be made NOW from decisions that are better made LATER with more context, and deliberately defer the second kind WHILE actively feeding it. Deferral funded by aggressive info-gathering, not passivity.
- **When it fails (critical):** it inverts into fatal dithering when the decision DECAYS (the window closes, the deal rots, someone dies while you "gather more"). Klein's point is experts act FAST on recognition, enlightened procrastination is only correct when time is genuinely on your side. Mistaking a now-decision for a later-decision is how you miss the market. Requires the discipline to actually decide when illumination comes.
- **For us:** sort every pending decision into "must decide now" vs "will be obviously right in two weeks if I keep learning," and stop forcing the second kind. But name an EXPIRY date on each deferral, founders rot deals and hires by "developing" a situation that needed a decision Monday. Agents are the ideal develop-the-situation engine: they saturate continuously so the call reaches the human near-illumination. (This is the same ground-then-act discipline as `alloro-forge`.)

## 4. Don't get treed by a chihuahua `[Documented]`
- **Framing:** don't let a small, misread threat make you over-react, under-react, or freeze, all three are deciding without context. "Connecting the dots before you've collected the dots."
- **Story:** deep into a brutal Delta-selection land-nav march (hour 15+, exhausted), his brain pattern-matched a shape into a bear, he ran, hit a drop-off, and jumped ~100 feet down a cliff, nearly ending his life. The "bear" was a pig. A tiny harmless input treed him because a depleted mind fabricated a pattern reality did not support.
- **Judgment-pattern:** under stress and fatigue the brain manufactures false patterns and jumps to a big reaction. Force a pause to COLLECT context before CONNECTING it, verify the threat before spending a huge irreversible response on it.
- **When it fails:** against a REAL bear, "collect more dots" gets you eaten. The twin risk is under-reaction (dismissing a real bear as "just a chihuahua"). The discipline is RIGHT-sizing the response to verified threat size, not reflexively down-sizing.
- **For us:** small companies get treed constantly, one angry tweet, one competitor launch, one churned logo triggers an all-hands panic pivot (a cliff-jump at a pig). Before a big irreversible reaction, collect the dots: how many customers actually, signal or noise. The founder's stress state is exactly the "hour 15" that manufactures bears. An agent that alerts on every anomaly TRAINS humans to jump off cliffs, tune agents to right-size alerts and collect dots before escalating.

## 5. It's not reality unless it's shared `[Documented]` (and the tragedy that proves it)
- **Framing:** individual situational awareness is worthless to the team unless circulated. Move from "need to know" to "need to share." Communicate boundarylessly, ACROSS the org chart, so the group catches one person's misread before it becomes action.
- **The tragedy (Takur Ghar / Roberts Ridge) `[Documented]`:** during Anaconda, higher wanted to insert a small SEAL element onto a hostile mountaintop. Blaber's AFO teams had the ground context and a better plan, but Blaber was cut out of the comms loop (a general reportedly had the satellite radio frequencies switched, severing the elements). A helicopter tried to land on an occupied peak, Neil Roberts fell from the aircraft, and a chain of deaths followed (John Chapman and others). Remote authority overrode the guy on the ground and the shared reality was deliberately broken. His critique, called even by critics "devastating and accurate," is that this was a SYSTEMIC failure of shared reality, not bad luck.
- **Judgment-pattern:** distributed knowledge becomes collective intelligence only if information flows freely and horizontally, any node that hoards or severs the flow degrades everyone's decisions, most dangerously when that node has the most authority and the least context.
- **When it fails:** "share everything" has a cost, total broadcast creates noise, slows tempo, or leaks. "Need to share" is a bias, not an absolute, share what changes others' decisions. And shared reality can converge on a shared WRONG answer (groupthink) if no one is charged with dissent.
- **For us:** in a near-flat 3-person company, lean into it, everyone in the same reality, no info hoarded by the founder as control. The brutal Takur Ghar lesson: **the person with the most authority and the least ground context is the one most likely to break shared reality and force a bad call from the top, and that is usually the founder.** For agents: the whole value of an agent mesh is shared reality, agents writing to a common context the humans can inspect. An agent (or founder) acting on private state no teammate can see is a Takur Ghar waiting to happen.

## 6. Plans are nothing, planning is everything / the footprint paradox `[Documented]`
- **Framing:** a rigid plan is a snapshot that dies on contact, the planning PROCESS (continuously developing the situation) is what has value. And accept intelligent risk rather than engineering all risk out.
- **Stories:** at Tora Bora, cruise-missile strikes meant to help actually HURT, they spooked bin Laden into ditching his satellite phone, destroying the only empirical thread on his location, a "safe" standoff action degraded the mission. The "footprint paradox": risk-averse doctrine deploys massive force to mitigate danger, but the huge footprint itself creates noise, signature, and slowness that make the mission harder. At Anaconda he chose the riskier-LOOKING quiet overland insertion over the "safe" default, precisely because it preserved surprise and context.
- **Judgment-pattern:** optimize for the actual MISSION, not the appearance of safety. Over-engineering against risk often TRANSFERS risk to the mission (bigger footprint, lost surprise, spooked target). Real risk management is choosing WHICH risks to accept for the goal.
- **When it fails:** this is not "risk is good." Audacity untethered from #3 and #4 is just recklessness. Intelligent risk is accepted BECAUSE you developed the situation, not to look bold. Applied wrong, "plans are nothing" excuses never planning.
- **For us:** small teams over-index on LOOKING safe (endless planning, big "proper" launches, hedging). The elaborate de-risked plan is often slower and worse than a quiet fast move that keeps you close to the truth. Plan hard (the process), hold the plan loosely (the artifact), take the intelligent risk the safety-theater version of you would refuse.

## 7. Imagine the unimaginable `[Documented]`
- **Framing:** "outrageous thoughts are barrier busters." Victory comes from out-THINKING, not out-numbering. Deliberately entertain absurd options, that is where the move your enemy can't model comes from. Run it defensively too, 9/11 succeeded partly because no one imagined it.
- **Stories:** planning a war-crimes-target capture in Bosnia, his team seriously brainstormed unconventional ambushes, including one featuring a gorilla suit, to break their own conventional pattern. Phantom parachutes rigged to melting ice blocks over Kandahar, seeding "American commandos are everywhere." A burkha infiltration concept exploiting the enemy's own cultural rules.
- **Judgment-pattern:** deliberately generate options outside the obvious solution space, including the ridiculous, because the highest-leverage move is usually the one the competitor's model can't predict, and the deadliest threat is the one you never imagined. Imagination as a discipline, run offense (find the unexpected move) AND defense (pre-mortem the unimaginable attack).
- **When it fails:** imagination without #3 and #6 is fantasy, the gorilla suit is brainstorming, not the executed plan. The failure mode is falling in love with the clever idea and executing it when a boring option was correct. Imagination widens the option set, judgment still chooses, and often chooses the mundane.
- **For us:** offense, a 3-person team can't out-resource anyone, so it must out-IMAGINE (the wedge/channel/mechanic a bigger competitor's model can't predict). Defense, "imagine the unimaginable" is a pre-mortem discipline, force yourself and your agents to name the failure no one is planning for (the dependency that dies, the platform that bans you). Agents are strong divergent generators, task one to produce the outrageous option set and the pre-mortem catastrophes a busy founder won't think of.

## 8. Ego is the enemy of good decisions / common sense as the OS `[Documented]`
- **Framing:** pride and hubris break common sense. Divorce the decision from your emotions and self-image so you can let in enabling information from unexpected, low-status, or unwelcome sources, even when it contradicts what you already committed to. His post-military banner is literally "The Common Sense Way": tell the truth when uncomfortable, take accountability for failures, decide from sensory reality, change your mind when new info demands it.
- **The pattern across every story:** ego is the hidden failure mode behind all the negatives, the commander who won't listen to the ground (#2), breaks shared reality to protect his plan (#5), over-reacts to a chihuahua to look decisive (#4), forces a snapshot to appear in control (#3), takes flashy risk to look bold (#6). "Me last" (#1) is the STRUCTURAL fix, ego-suppression is the IN-THE-MOMENT fix.
- **Judgment-pattern:** the quality of a decision is capped by the leader's willingness to be shown wrong. Ego filters out exactly the disconfirming information you most need, so manufacture humility, actively seek the input that would prove you wrong, update on sensory reality over self-image.
- **When it fails (honest limit):** ego-lessness has a floor, a leader also needs the CONFIDENCE to make the hard call and hold the line against a bad order (the Iraq withdrawal, the Anaconda argument). Pure ego-suppression tips into indecision or being bullied by the loudest voice. And a documented critique of Blaber himself: reviewers note he applies his accountability standard SELECTIVELY (harsh on some leaders, lenient on politically aligned ones). Even the mentor doesn't perfectly live the principle, which IS the lesson, ego is the permanent adversary, not a solved problem.
- **For us:** in a 3-person company the founder's ego has no counterweight, no board in the room, no peer to push back in the moment. Build the counterweight deliberately, reward the teammate (or agent) that shows you you're wrong, treat "I changed my mind on new information" as strength not flip-flopping, separate "this decision failed" from "I am a failure" so you can reverse fast.

## The mentor's-eye summary (the calibration, not the slogan)
Each principle has a deferral/humility virtue that, over-applied, becomes its own vice:
- Develop-the-situation → dithering. Listen-to-the-ground → abdication. Chihuahua-caution → missing the real bear. Share-everything → noise/groupthink. Imagine → fantasy. Accept-risk → recklessness. Me-last → self-neglect. Ego-less → spineless.

A mentor teaches WHERE the line is for each, in this situation. That is what makes the profile mentor-grade instead of a name-drop.
