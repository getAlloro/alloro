# The North Star

Alloro exists to give local service business owners back their freedom. The owners we serve trained for years in their craft, opened a business to do what they love, and accidentally bought a second job they never wanted: marketing, websites, reviews, Google, referrals, all of it. Alloro's job is to take that load off so they can do what they're great at.

The category we are creating is **Business Clarity**. Not analytics. Not marketing. Not reputation management. Business Clarity is the experience of an owner knowing, in 30 seconds, that their business is healthy, where it's leaking if it is, and what to do about it this week.

Two North Stars, locked:

1. Undeniable value for every customer. Real and felt.
2. Alloro maintains a path to becoming an inevitable unicorn.

The Standard from Claude's Corner sits above both: *Does it make a human feel understood before it makes them feel informed?*

# The Owner We Serve

Local service business owners share five universal problems, regardless of vertical:

1. They can't see what's happening in their business
2. They're doing jobs they never trained for
3. They're paying for things they can't measure
4. They're running it alone
5. They're always behind

Endodontist, orthodontist, chiropractor, physical therapist, optometrist, attorney, CPA, vet, financial advisor. The vocabulary differs by vertical. The problem is identical.

**What the owner thinks they need:** Better marketing. Higher rankings. More leads. More followers. The agency that finally delivers.

**What the owner actually needs:** A way to know their business is healthy without having to learn marketing, SEO, or analytics. A signal when something drifts. A single recommendation for the one thing to focus on this week. Permission to stop watching the metrics that don't matter.

**How owners make decisions:** By feel, calibrated against external signals. They look at their schedule (busy or not), they look at money in the bank (up or down), and they look at the most recent agency report (good or bad). When those three signals align, they relax. When they don't align, they panic and start changing things. The owner's anxiety is not about data. It's about confidence in the trend.

Alloro's job is to be the fourth signal that resolves disagreements between the other three. When schedule, bank, and agency report point in different directions, Alloro tells them which signal to trust this week.

# The Five Stages

Local service business owners think their business runs on inputs (rankings, ads, traffic). It actually runs on a five-stage customer journey. Most of what owners pay for affects only one or two stages. Most of what owners watch on dashboards is one or two metrics inside one or two stages. The whole picture rarely gets named.

The five stages, in the customer's voice.

## Stage 1: Awareness

> *"I have a problem."*

The customer realizes they have a problem the owner can solve. Tooth pain. Broken AC. Sore back. Leaky roof.

This stage is **not Alloro's job**. The customer either has the problem or they don't. Demand exists outside Alloro's control. Ads can amplify it. Content can prime it. But the moment of "I have a problem" happens in the customer's life, not on the owner's website.

Owners who pay Alloro to "create more demand" are paying for the wrong thing. The right question is: *of the people who already have the problem, how many are finding us, choosing us, booking, and coming back?*

## Stage 2: Research → Be Findable

> *"Who can help me?"*

The customer searches. Google, Apple Maps, social media, asks a friend. They surface a list of options.

**Alloro's job at this stage: Be Findable.** The owner needs to show up when the search happens. The mechanisms include Google Business Profile completeness, local SEO, reviews, photos, citations, and Google's algorithm dynamics around proximity, relevance, and prominence.

**Metrics that live here:** local ranking, profile views, search impressions, share of voice within local search results, photo views.

**Common leaks:**

- Profile is incomplete or outdated
- Photo count is low or photos are stale
- Review count is below local market median
- Categories are mis-set
- Competitors recently surged on reviews

**What owners think this stage is about:** ranking #1.

**What it's actually about:** showing up in the top set of options. Being in the top three results is fundamentally different from being #1, but the business consequences are roughly the same. Owners over-optimize for the #1 vs #2 distinction and under-optimize for being in the top set vs. not being in the top set at all.

## Stage 3: Consideration → Be Choosable

> *"Which one should I go with?"*

The customer has a list. Now they're comparing. They click profiles, read reviews, look at photos, scan the website.

**Alloro's job at this stage: Be Choosable.** The owner needs to win the comparison. The mechanisms include review quality and quantity, photo quality, website first impression, professional credibility, and any signal of trust or specialty.

**Metrics that live here:** profile clicks, website visits from profile, time on site, pages viewed, bounce rate, before-and-after photo engagement.

**Common leaks:**

- Website looks dated or unprofessional
- Reviews exist but don't communicate the specialty or differentiation
- Photos are generic stock images
- No clear sense of who the doctor or owner is as a person

Most local service businesses lose more revenue at this stage than anywhere else. The customer found them but chose someone else. This is the most expensive stage to lose because the marketing dollars to be findable have already been spent.

## Stage 4: Decision → Be Bookable

> *"Let me book this."*

The customer has decided. Now they need to convert intent into appointment. They call, they fill out a form, they book online.

**Alloro's job at this stage: Be Bookable.** The owner needs to make the conversion friction-free. The mechanisms include phone answering practices, online scheduling, response time to web forms, ease of finding the phone number, and clarity of the call-to-action.

**Metrics that live here:** phone calls from profile, form submissions, online bookings, conversion rate from website visit to appointment, after-hours capture rate.

**Common leaks:**

- Phone goes to voicemail during business hours
- Form submissions take 24+ hours to respond
- Online booking link is broken or hidden
- The call-to-action is generic ("learn more") instead of decisive ("book now")

This is the most fixable stage. A broken booking flow is solved in a week with the right attention. But owners rarely notice it because the symptom is "fewer patients" rather than "broken booking link." Alloro's job is to make it noticeable.

## Stage 5: Loyalty → Be Memorable

> *"I'll come back. I'll tell a friend."*

The customer became a patient. Now they decide whether to return, refer, review.

**Alloro's job at this stage: Be Memorable.** The owner needs the customer to remember them, recommend them, and write about them. The mechanisms include service quality, follow-up communications, review requests, referral programs, and the patient experience inside the office.

**Metrics that live here:** review velocity (new reviews per month), review sentiment, repeat visit rate, referral source share, NPS or similar satisfaction signal.

**Common leaks:**

- No systematic ask for reviews
- Existing customers aren't told they can refer
- Follow-up communications are generic or missing
- Patient experience inside the office doesn't match the marketing outside

This stage compounds. A happy patient creates more demand in Stage 1 (their friend now has a problem and remembers them), more findability in Stage 2 (more reviews), more credibility in Stage 3 (better reviews, more recent), and more conversion in Stage 4 (warm referral converts at 2-3x cold).

# The Translation Layer

Every metric Alloro shows needs a sentence in plain language explaining what it means and what to do about it.

## Voice Principles

- The owner is the hero. Alloro is the guide.
- No jargon. No "SERP," "DA," "GBP," or "SEO" without explanation.
- No agency-speak. No "synergies," "engagement," "thought leadership."
- Plain English in the owner's voice. "You're showing up when patients search."
- Action-oriented. "Ask three patients for reviews this week."
- Trend-focused. "Up from last week" beats "23% above benchmark."

## Examples

**Bad translation:** *Local SEO ranking: #2 in proximity-weighted search results.*

**Good translation:** *You're showing up #2 when patients search for endodontists near Falls Church. Up from #3 last week. The driver: 8 new reviews. Keep going.*

**Bad translation:** *Bounce rate: 67%.*

**Good translation:** *Most people who visit your website leave without clicking anything. The bottleneck is probably your homepage. Try a clearer call-to-action.*

**Bad translation:** *GBP Conversion Rate: 4.2%.*

**Good translation:** *Of every 100 people who see your Google profile, 4 are booking or calling. Industry average is 6. The leak is between people finding you and people booking. Most likely cause: your reviews say you're great but your website doesn't show why.*

The translation layer is mostly written content, not code. It's a discipline that lives in tooltips, daily summaries, recommendations, and onboarding scripts. Once locked, it doesn't change unless the underlying mechanic changes.

# The Action Layer

Every week, every customer should see one thing at the top of their dashboard: the one thing to focus on this week. Generated by the Intelligence Agent. Anchored in the weakest stage or the biggest drift. Delivered in one sentence.

## Principles for a Recommendation

- Names one stage explicitly (Findable, Choosable, Bookable, Memorable)
- States what's leaking and why
- Specifies the action (verb + object + when)
- Estimates the expected outcome (optional, but adds confidence)

## Examples

*Your booking conversion dropped 22% at the Falls Church location this week. The likely cause: a broken Schedule Online button. Have the staff click it on three devices today.*

*You're losing the Choosable stage in Fredericksburg. Three competitors added more recent reviews than you in the past 30 days. Ask three patients this week to leave a review.*

*Your Memorable stage is your strongest. You added 12 reviews this month. Now is the time to ask your three highest-LTV patients for a referral.*

## What NOT to Do

- Never show more than one recommendation at a time. Cognitive overload destroys clarity.
- Never recommend something the owner can't act on this week. No "rebrand your practice."
- Never recommend something whose outcome can't be measured within 30 days.
- Never use technical language (no "optimize for E-E-A-T").
- Never frame a problem without naming the action.

The recommendation is the product. Everything else is supporting evidence.

# The Undeniable Value Test

Three tests, applied progressively.

## The 30-Second Test

Can the owner open Alloro, look at one screen, and answer *"is my business healthy and what should I do this week?"*

If yes, the product is delivering at the surface level. If no, the dashboard is failing.

## The 30-Day Test

Has the owner taken at least one action because of an Alloro recommendation, and seen a measurable change?

If yes, the product has earned a place in their weekly rhythm. If no, the recommendations are not landing.

## The Saif-to-Chris Test

Has the customer moved from *"explain to me why this number is what it is"* to *"just tell me what to do this week"*?

If yes, trust has shifted. If no, the product is still in verification mode and is at risk of churn the moment the owner is busy or stressed.

Every product change, every marketing message, every onboarding moment gets measured against these three tests. They are the operationalized version of Undeniable Value.

# What We Don't Do

The [Owner.com](http://Owner.com) bet, applied to Alloro: *we won't show you everything. We'll tell you the one thing.*

Things Alloro deliberately does not promise:

- We don't promise to create demand. Demand exists outside our control. We help convert the demand that exists.
- We don't promise rankings as the outcome. Rankings are an input. We promise to surface the right inputs to optimize for the outcomes you care about.
- We don't promise everything you might want to see. We promise the things you actually need to act on.
- We don't promise endless customization. We promise a proven model applied to your business.

This is the trade we ask owners to accept: less control, more clarity. Less data, more confidence. Less analytics, more action.

Customers who don't accept this trade will not be a fit for Alloro. That's okay. The trade is the product.

# Anti-Patterns

When the team is unsure what to do, run the proposed change through these anti-patterns. If it matches any of them, reject the change.

- Adding a metric to the dashboard without writing its translation sentence first
- Adding a feature that requires the owner to learn something new to use
- Adding configurability or customization where a sensible default could exist
- Making the dashboard look different than it did last week
- Building a feature that only matters for one specific customer's edge case
- Generating recommendations the customer can't act on this week
- Using language that requires the customer to know marketing jargon

Each of these is a Saif-mode reinforcement. Each is a step away from undeniable value.
